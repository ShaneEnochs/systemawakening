Probably.

This is what you know: the treasury is painfully thin, and the tenants down in the valley are cautious folks. You are riding into a village that doesn't know you, to rule a people who don't yet trust you. But the cold air is yours to breathe, and the land is yours to rule. Every decision from this day forward is local, personal, and entirely upon your shoulders.

// Beat 1: The Steward's Assessment

The carriage leaves you at the heavy doors of Pinehaven Hall. Inside, the dark, local stone is bitter cold, refusing to hold whatever meager heat the hearth provides. The formal study is immaculately swept in a desperate attempt to maintain appearances. Yet, the threadbare rugs and the draft rattling the glass panes betray the truth of your father's... well, now your ruined estate.

Standing beside the scarred oak desk is your steward, Mr. Thorne. You remember him from your childhood as a looming, humorless phantom who chased you from the kitchens and scolded you for tracking mud onto the rugs. He was a villain of your childhood, but looking at him now, the malice you once imagined is gone.

He is a severe, sharp-eyed man with a rigid spine. Your late father was a man of... shall we say, [i]leisure[/i], far more interested in the bottom of a sherry glass and weeks-long hunting excursions on the moors than the actual business of governance. With the baron constantly absent or inebriated, you now know it fell entirely to Mr. Thorne to keep the ${Family} household from collapsing.

He wears a tailored black wool coat that might have been fashionable three decades ago, meticulously brushed to hide the fraying threads at the cuffs. As he grips several heavy leather-bound books, the ledgers, you assume, you catch a glimpse of the dark ink stains set deep into his knuckles. Through the eyes of an adult inheriting a ruined estate, you think you finally understand him. All those years ago, he wasn't being cruel; he was simply exhausted, and those stained hands were the ones that have been physically holding this bleeding barony together the entire time.

Mr. Thorne presents the estate ledgers with a slight, rigid bow. "Welcome home, ${Title}," he says, setting the heavy volumes on the desk.

"Mr. Thorne, please, call me ${Name}," you say. "You've known me since I was born."

Thorne somehow stiffens even further, his sharp eyes narrowing. "That would be entirely inappropriate, my {lord}. You must adopt the mindset of your station. You are no longer a child running underfoot. You are above the help, chosen by God to rule this land."

The elderly man merely shakes his head, sighs, and taps the leather-bound books. "I have tallied the accounts."

You stare at the thick covers. "And I assume you are not bringing me good news."

"The winter crops are struggling," he replies, almost sounding embarrassed. "The soil in the valley is exhausted, so the yield will likely be quite poor."

You let out a slow breath, the cold air of the study biting at your lungs. "Alright. And my father's debts?"

Thorne clasp his hands rigidly behind his back. "Yes. Well, my {lord}, the creditors' preliminary inquiries have already begun to arrive, and they are aware how heavily encumbered the estate truly is."

He lets the meaning of his words sink in for you. If the creditors know that the truth, that the coffers are nearly empty and crops are failing, they won't wait for the meager winter harvest to fail. "However," he adds, his voice dropping to a smooth, accommodating murmur. "I remain at your complete disposal to manage these daily minutiae, should you wish to focus on broader matters."
