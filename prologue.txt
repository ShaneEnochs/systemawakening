*title [Forward] A Mostly Accurate Retelling

If you're reading this, it means that we were successful in our mission to time travel, but we were wrong in thinking that we would keep our memories of the alternate timeline. Thankfully, Callie (yes, [i]that[/i] Callie) is a genius and figured out how to store this data safely, so you can read about what happened the first time around and get a leg up on the things that are to come that we wish we had.

I'll start at the very beginning, since otherwise you won't have the context for the really big things that happen later. Also, don't worry about the length of time that it takes to read through this message. Callie said that while you have this screen open, you'll be in some kind of time dilation where for every hour you experience, less than a second passes outside. She tried explaining it to me a few times, but you know how Callie is when she tries to dumb things down for us.

Anyway, here's how it all happened.

*title [Chapter 1] The Hargrove Institute

The campus of the Hargrove Institute for System Studies is... underwhelming. Stepping down from the bus, you genuinely wonder if you've been dropped off at some remote satellite location rather than the main campus. The grounds consist of a handful of brick buildings surrounded by tidy landscaping and completely lacking any and all grandeur you had anticipated from this school.

As one of the country's few System academies, finding authentic photos of the place was utterly impossible. Every publicly available satellite image was blurred and remarkably the Internet didn't seem to have any pictures either. The mystery behind this place had created an obviously false image in your head over the years, because what you had been picturing was closer to an imposing stone castle situated across an ancient lake, complete with floating candles in a dining hall and eccentric professors giving warnings about scary forests and forbidden floors.

Sadly, there's not a single pointy hat in sight.

You spent six years bleeding for the chance to attend the Harvard of System academies, but the reality standing in front of you looks remarkably like a mundane community college.

Regardless, you're finally here, and you feel...

*page_break "Deal!"

You have known Callie since sophomore year of high school.

During the first few weeks of Introduction to Electronics, she was just the remarkably quiet girl sitting two benches over. She was always punctual, taking her seat before the bell even rang, and she kept her workspace aggressively neat in a room otherwise filled with tangled wire and bad solder. Everything she did was deliberate.

You honestly did not think much of it at the time.

*page_break

The assignment that day was simple enough, designed primarily to build confidence before the difficult material began. Mr. Otieno had walked the class through it twice, instructing everyone to build a basic circuit on a breadboard where a switch controls a light.

You were about halfway through the process and second-guessing your resistor placement when Mr. Otieno abruptly stopped pacing the room.

He was standing completely still at Callie's bench.

She was already finished, but her breadboard looked absolutely nothing like the simple diagram provided on the worksheet. Instead of a modest tangle of jumper wire, she had constructed something resembling a miniature, highly organized city. Every single wire was cut to the exact length required, and every component was aligned perfectly parallel or perpendicular to the board. The light was not just switched on; it was actively tracking her hand's movement and smoothly shifting its brightness in response.

Mr. Otieno stared at the creation for a long moment.

"Miss Mercer," he finally said. "Would you like to explain what this is?"

Callie looked up with faint surprise, almost as if she had forgotten the rest of the room existed for the last forty minutes.

"It's the assignment," she replied evenly. "Mostly."

*page_break

She proceeded to walk him through the entire build, though she might as well have been speaking Greek. You completely lost the thread within the first thirty seconds as she casually discussed passive infrared sensors and the inherent inefficiency of binary switching in low-voltage applications. She was not trying to show off or speak above anyone's head. She just explained her reasoning the same way you would describe your daily commute, assuming everyone else could clearly see the exact same roads.

Mr. Otieno nodded along with the careful, deliberate rhythm of a man desperately trying to buy time.

The rest of the class had completely stopped working to watch the exchange.

"Right," Mr. Otieno said eventually, straightening up and stepping back. "Yes. That's... yes. Full marks, Miss Mercer. Obviously."

Callie accepted this as a completely reasonable outcome and immediately went back to aligning her unused components into a precise little row.

You looked down at your own breadboard to find your resistor plugged in backward and your solder completely cold.

When you glanced back over at her, she was fully packed up four minutes before the bell, leaving her workspace exactly as she found it.

That was the true beginning of it.

*page_break

The thing you kept coming back to in those early days was the simple fact that she was never mean about her intelligence.

She was just uncomfortably, relentlessly honest. When she told a girl in English class that her essay argument was logically circular, she did it unprompted but without any malice. She delivered the critique the same way someone might point out a piece of spinach stuck in your teeth, viewing it as a genuinely helpful service.

When a group of seniors invited her to their lunch table, she politely declined by explaining that making conversation made it too difficult to focus on chewing her food. They stood there looking as though they had just been slapped, but Callie completely lacked any sense of superiority. There was absolutely no posturing or performance in her behavior. She just stated the truth and always seemed baffled when it landed badly with other people.

*page_break

She arrived at every destination early, ate lunch alone at the exact same table by the window, and always brought the exact same sandwich cut into four perfect triangles.

She did not appear lonely, or if she was, she was completely unaware of the feeling.

You easily could have ignored her the way everyone else did.

*label callie_payoff

It took a few weeks of consistent lunches to finally establish a comfortable rhythm together.

Callie completely ignored the concept of small talk because she genuinely did not understand its purpose, a fact she stated directly the very first time you tried asking about her weekend.

She pointed out that asking generic questions was a very long way around actually having a conversation, suggesting you should just talk to her if you had something to say.

So you took her advice and talked about the things that actually interested you. She listened with an intense, focused attention that you eventually realized was just her standard way of processing information. She never faked her interest, meaning you always knew exactly when she was genuinely invested in a topic.

She also remembered the smallest details about you, like your absolute hatred of ice in your drinks and your strange habit of tapping a pen three times before writing. She never explicitly mentioned noticing these quirks; she simply accommodated them without a word.

By the time junior year started, she had become your closest friend. It happened the same way most important things do: gradually at first, and then all at once.

*page_break

A sharp whistle cuts across the lawn, dragging your attention violently back to the present moment.

A woman stands near the main building with two fingers pressed to her lips, radiating the amused energy of an administrator who has corralled confused freshmen a hundred times before. She wears the universal costume of institutional authority, complete with a heavy clipboard and a branded lanyard.

"New students!" she calls out across the grass. "Over here, please. Leave your bags where they are, and they will be brought directly to your rooms."

Callie straightens up so fast that all three of her heavy duffels slide dangerously off her shoulders. She manages to catch two of them, but the third hits the grass with a heavy thud, and she simply leaves it behind.

"Coming," she says to nobody in particular.

You fall into step beside her as the twenty or so new arrivals slowly converge, all of you silently sizing each other up without wanting to be caught staring.

The woman introduces herself as Director Osei without bothering to stop her brisk walk toward the building's entrance. She speaks with the practiced cadence of someone who is entirely used to rooms going completely silent the moment she opens her mouth.

"Welcome to Hargrove. I know exactly what it looks like from out here," she says, letting a brief pause hang in the air. "It's supposed to."

*page_break

The building looks exactly like an uninspired administrative block from the outside, featuring beige brick walls and completely drawn blinds. A remarkably plain sign beside the door reads RECORDS AND ENROLLMENT.

Inside, you find a short corridor containing an empty reception desk, a dying potted fern, and a linoleum floor that clicks sharply under your boots.

Director Osei pushes her way through a set of double doors at the end of the hall without slowing down.

You follow her directly into a room that contains absolutely nothing.

There are no windows, no pieces of furniture, and no other doors leading out. It is just bare concrete walls, a matching ceiling, and a floor featuring a subtle seam that runs around the edge of the room in a perfect rectangle.

The double doors swing shut behind the very last student with a heavy, final thunk as the locks engage.

"Feet together, please," Director Osei commands. "Step away from the edges."

*page_break

The entire floor suddenly drops out from under you.

It is not a terrifying freefall, but rather a smooth and massive descent that leaves your stomach lagging a half-second behind the rest of your body. Someone in the back of the group lets out a startled gasp as the concrete walls slide rapidly upward, making you realize this was never a room at all. It is a massive elevator shaft.

The daylight filtering in from above shrinks from a bright rectangle to a thin line before vanishing entirely.

As you pass the third and fourth floors, a pale amber glow begins rising from the depths to meet the descending platform.

Five floors underground, the massive elevator slows its descent and comes to a complete halt with a deep, satisfied hum.

The air down here feels entirely different against your skin. It is remarkably cool and still, carrying the heavy, ozone-rich scent of an impending thunderstorm that has been tightly compressed into the space. Whatever facility exists down here is clearly drawing a massive amount of power.

*page_break

The underground hallway looks absolutely nothing like the mundane administrative building resting on the surface.

The arched ceiling sits several meters high and is lined with glowing amber panels that pulse softly like a slow heartbeat. The dark stone walls are dressed in sleek metal conduits and thick cable lines, creating an atmosphere that feels simultaneously ancient and brutally functional. Your footsteps fail to echo on the polished concrete floor, the sound simply swallowed up by the vast space.

Director Osei leads the group forward in complete silence.

Callie suddenly appears right at your elbow.

"Okay," she whispers into the quiet space. "I take it back. This is definitely more."

You glance over to see her staring straight ahead, completely failing to hide a massive grin.

The hallway curves gently to the right, following the natural shape of the bedrock, before opening up entirely.

*page_break

The underground auditorium is carved directly into the living rock and looks large enough to seat at least three hundred people. Tiered seating descends steeply toward a wide circular stage, while an enormous, glowing amber seal hangs suspended above the space. It proudly displays the name of the Hargrove Institute for System Studies.

The Latin phrase engraved underneath the seal does not translate to anything simple or inspirational like knowledge or excellence.

It translates to: [i]What hunts must first learn to be still.[/i]

Director Osei takes the stage without any fanfare, having abandoned her clipboard somewhere along the way. She clearly does not need it down here.

"Sit," she instructs the group. "And listen carefully, because I will only say this once."
