The campus of the Hargrove Institute for System Studies is... underwhelming. Stepping down from the bus, I genuinely wonder if I've been dropped off at some remote satellite location rather than the main campus. The grounds consist of a handful of brick buildings surrounded by tidy landscaping and completely lacking any and all grandeur I had anticipated from this school.

As one of the country's few System academies, finding authentic photos of the place was utterly impossible. Every publicly available satellite image was blurred and remarkably the Internet didn't seem to have any pictures either. The mystery behind this place had created an obviously false image in my head over the years, because what I had been picturing was closer to an imposing stone castle situated across an ancient lake, complete with floating candles in a dining hall and eccentric professors giving warnings about scary forests and forbidden floors.

Sadly, there's not a single pointy hat in sight.

I spent six years bleeding for the chance to attend the Harvard of System academies, but the reality standing in front of me looks remarkably like a mundane community college.

Regardless, I'm finally here, and I...

"Callie!" I say, my voice rising a notch. "We agreed to wait. We were supposed to pick our classes together."

"I know, I know," she says, wincing. "But I honestly couldn't have gotten it running otherwise, and I needed it to run."

I let out a long breath, fighting back my irritation. I do understand. The alternative would have been sitting in a cramped car with her dad for hours. His car may not have been able to make the trip, and she probably would have had to trigger it anyway. "What level are you?"

"Just level one," she says quickly, holding up both hands. "I promise. I literally just locked in the class and fixed the car. I haven't done anything else."

The tension bleeds out of my shoulders. "Guessing you chose Artificer?"

A genuine, bright grin breaks through her guilt. "You know it."

[b]One Year Ago[/b]

I knew Callie since my senior year of high school, where she spent the first few weeks of Introduction to Electronics as just a quiet girl sitting two benches over. She always claimed her seat before the bell rang and maintained an aggressively neat workspace in a shop choked with tangled wires and rogue solder drops, but otherwise faded into the background.

And then came the first hands-on assignment.

Designed primarily to build confidence before the difficult material began, the initial task required everyone to follow a diagram and build a basic circuit where a manual switch controlled a single LED light.

I was halfway through the process and actively second-guessing my enrollment in the class when Mr. Ortega let out a low whistle at Callie's bench. She had already finished, but her workstation lacked anything resembling the standard circuit board layout. She had instead constructed a rapidly spinning LED light bar, powered by a tiny motor and battery she had found. As the contraption whirred, the lights projected a perfect, glowing top-down schematic of the exact basic circuit the rest of the class struggled to assemble.

Mr. Ortega, the rest of the class, and I stared at the hovering projection for a long while.

"Miss Owens," Mr. Ortega finally said. "Would you like to explain what this is?"

Callie shifted under all of the attention.

"It is the assignment," she replied. "Well, mostly."

She walked him through the entire build, though she might as well have been speaking Greek to the rest of the room. I lost the thread within the first ten seconds as she explained motor synchronization, refresh rates, and how she calculated the rotational timing required to stabilize the projected image. She wasn't trying to show off, even if I first assumed she was. Rather, that's just how Callie's mind worked. When given a task, she couldn't help but take it to the next level. Or sometimes twenty levels ahead, as in that particular case.

Mr. Ortega nodded along until she finished. "Right," he said, straightening up and stepping back from her bench. "Yes. That is... well that's really great work. Full marks. Obviously."

I remember looking at her and feeling...

The two of us were pretty much inseparable after that day.

We were opposites in almost every way, but it somehow just worked. She shared that she had a burning dream of becoming the world's first S-tier Artificer. She wanted to build inventions that would pull the world into a new era of prosperity and peace. I, on the other hand, wanted to fight. My dream was to stand shoulder to shoulder with the rankers who saved the world from the titanic creatures bent on seeing every human dead.

We made a pact to help each other reach those dreams, no matter what. When she got too far into the zone, I forced her to focus on one project at a time. I also made sure she remembered to eat. In return, she helped me study and stepped in as a surprisingly capable sparring partner.
