You are carried upstairs.

This is not a metaphor. Your legs don't work properly yet — something about the ritual's transfer, Stella explains in a low voice as two soldiers take your arms, is that the body needs time to accept the soul that has entered it. The original tenant burned away cleanly. The new one — you — is still settling.

The Baron's chambers are on the third floor of the keep. The bed is wide and cold and smells faintly of cedar and old stone. Someone has laid a fire. The flames do almost nothing for the chill.

Stella draws a chair to your bedside and produces a small notebook. She has the brisk manner of a physician who has already decided the patient will survive and is now focused on getting them functional.

"You're going to have questions," she says. "I'll answer what I can. But first I need to assess what came through intact."

She holds up three fingers.

"How many?"

"Three," you say. Your voice is wrong. Too deep, rougher than you remember. [i]His[/i] voice.

Stella makes a note. "Good. Language is intact." She pauses. "Do you know where you are?"

She spends the next two hours briefing you. Some of it you absorb. Some of it slides off your mind like water on glass, too strange to stick.

The world has a [i]Framework[/i] — a vast, invisible architecture that governs ability, growth, and power. Everyone in this world has a stat sheet, a class, a measurable set of numbers that define what they can and cannot do. The King controls the Framework at an administrative level, which means he can read anyone's stats, alter the rules, and — in extreme cases — delete what he doesn't like.

Silas had a Framework interface like everyone else. His has been repurposed for you, with significant modifications Stella calls the Jailbreak. It hides your true abilities from external scrying. It is, she says, imperfect.

"How imperfect?" you ask.

"It glitches occasionally." She says it without inflection. "You may see data that doesn't belong to you. Ignore it. It will stabilize."

The lantern-girl from the ritual chamber brings a tray of food at some point. She says nothing. You eat mechanically, tasting nothing.

*page_break

Before Stella leaves, Hudson comes to the door. He stands in the frame as if entering would be a concession of some kind. His expression is the one you noticed in the ritual chamber — that held-in-between thing.

"How is —" he starts, then stops. "How are you?"

"Confused," you say.

He nods, once, as if this is an acceptable answer. "There will be a briefing tomorrow on the military situation. Get some rest."

He leaves before you can respond. The door closes. The fire pops.

You lie in Silas's bed, in Silas's body, and stare at Silas's ceiling, and try to remember what your ceiling looked like.

You fall asleep before you manage it.

*label day_three

Stella comes in the morning with two cups of something hot and her notebook, and spends three hours teaching you the Framework's basics.

Everyone has a stat sheet. Everyone has a class, assigned at adolescence based on aptitude and circumstances. Stats grow through use and through XP, which is awarded by the Framework itself for meaningful action — combat, leadership, significant choices. The King's Admin access lets him read anyone's sheet, grant or revoke classes, and in theory alter the underlying rules.

The Jailbreak Stella built into Silas's body — into [i]your[/i] body now — spoofs your sheet when it's scanned externally. Anyone who tries to read your stats sees a fabricated baseline. Unremarkable numbers for an unremarkable baron.

"What do my actual stats look like?" you ask.

She shows you. They are low. Noticeably low for someone who is supposed to be in command of an army.

"Silas's were higher," she says, without apology. "You'll grow into it. The Framework rewards action."
