*title [Prologue] Induction

The stack of papers you have to sign for the NDA is taller than your mortgage documents. You diligently read the first twenty pages or so, but skim the rest, eager to get started. The lawyer representing the VR game development company [i]All These Worlds[/i] frowns in clear disapproval as you sign and initial page after page without reading them, but NDAs usually boil down to "don't talk about it or we'll sue you." As you have no intention of doing that, you should be fine. Right?

You recline in the pod and glance over at your friend and coworker, Alex Landry, climbing into the pod beside yours. One hundred spots in the entire alpha, and somehow he managed to not only get one, but two of them. Apparently he had been sitting at a bar a couple weeks back, deep in misery over the latest breakup which had astonishingly lasted almost three weeks this time. He got to talking with the guy on the next stool over who turned out to be a developer at [i]All These Worlds[/i] and he, too, was finding some comfort at the bottom of a glass. 

They had spent the better part of three hours doing what people in that situation do: drink too much and tell a complete stranger things they probably shouldn't. But trauma bonding is a thing, and in this case, it worked out pretty well for the both of you because somewhere in the middle of all that, the developer had offered Alex a spot in the alpha. How (or why) your friend had then parlayed that offer into a second invite for you, you have no idea, but you will forever be thankful for it, and you'll probably owe him an infinite number of favors for the rest of both of your lives.

Alex grins at you as the nurses begin attaching electrodes to your forehead, chest, wrists, and ankles. When they finish, you eye the tangle of wires with mild unease.

"They're just so we can monitor your vitals while you're in there," says the nurse attending to you. He's a big guy, broad through the shoulders, with hands that look like they could palm a basketball without trying. You find yourself wondering if he played college ball, or maybe just never stopped lifting. "Nothing to worry about. This is completely safe."

He holds out a pinky.

*label induction

"Good morning, folks," he says, addressing you and the four other pod testers in the room. "Thanks for coming in. I know the NDA process was a headache, but we think we’ve built something incredible here and hopefully you'll agree once you're inside.

"A quick heads-up before we start," he continues. "You’re going to see a lot of placeholder text. 'Crimson City', 'Cinnabar Guard 4', stuff like that. Ignore it. The narrative team is still finalizing the lore and naming conventions, and it'll all be updated before beta testing. There are some quests available, but we don't care too much about the story right now. We want to test the physics, the economy, and the skill trees. We need you to push the boundaries and try to break the mechanics if you can.

"Usually, we’d isolate you to a single level or a specific combat loop, but as you'll see soon, Path of Empire doesn't work like that. The systems are too tangled and everything bleeds into everything else. If we split it up, the simulation just falls apart so we’re dropping you into the complete build and you'll have full run of the map.

"Whenever you're ready, go ahead and place your hands on the armrest basins. It's going to feel a little weird, but it's normal. Just keep your hands still, remember to breath, and we'll trigger the induction sequence."

The man, who still hasn't introduced himself, gives a winning smile as he looks each of you in the eye in turn.

"See you on the other side!"

*page_break

You place your hands on the two silver half-spheres at the ends of the armrests. They're cold at first, smooth and slightly yielding, like pressing your palms against chilled silicone. As your body heat seeps into them, your hands begin to sink into the material, and you feel a faint pins and needles sensation spread up your wrists and forearms.

You've read about the breakthroughs in nanomachines and how they've transformed military and medical applications. Somehow, [i]All These Worlds[/i] has secured FDA approval to use them in their pods for fully integrated VR. A first of its kind, the articles said. You hadn't really thought about what that meant until right now, with millions of microscopic machines working their way into your bloodstream and heading toward your brain.

The half-spheres slowly disappear as the nanites absorb your body heat and glucose (thank goodness for the chocolate chip cookie they gave you before coming in here) for energy, migrating between your skin cells and spreading through your entire system. You feel a deep chill follow them inward, like swallowing ice-cold water. The glass pod cover slides into place above you with a soft, pressurized hiss, sealing you inside. A woman's calm voice comes through the speakers on either side of your head.

"Induction process commencing. Please continue breathing normally. Induction in 3... 2... 1..."

*page_break

You expect some kind of transition. A fade, a loading screen, or maybe even a moment of darkness. There isn't one. 

One moment you're looking up through the glass canopy at the white ceiling of the induction room, and the next you're staring at the wooden interior of a carriage and feeling it bump as it travels. Your nose is assaulted with a host of unpleasant smells. The entire wooden frame of the carriage groans and squeaks and sways continuously, the iron-banded wheels rattle over deep mud ruts. Outside a small, grime-streaked window, a dense line of trees blurs past.

You spend the first few minutes just taking it all in. The sensory detail is staggering. You examine your hands, noting the dirt under your fingernails and the coarse weave of the wool pants you're wearing. The chill of the air feels real. You even have a dull ache in your lower back from sitting on the hard wooden bench.

A thought occurs to you. You have no idea how to interact with this world or see your stats. You attempt to mentally call up your system menu, and to your surprise, it actually works. A translucent screen pops into existence in front of your face.

You shift on the hard wooden bench, bracing yourself against the erratic swaying, and rap your knuckles against the small hatch at the front of the cabin. The wooden panel slides open.

A man in a heavy, soaked wool coat glances back at you, rainwater dripping from the wide brim of his leather hat.

"Uh, hey," you call out over the loud clatter of the iron wheels. "Are we heading to... Apricot City?"

The driver squints, looking at you like you're an idiot. "Apricot ain't no city, [i]my {lord},[/i]" he says in a slow, gravely voice. Yep, he definitely thinks you're an idiot. "Ain't hardly a village, neither. It's a fair piece north of here. We're making for the Wisteria barony. Same as we was when we set out."

"Right... And what's my role here?"

"You take a knock to the head?" he asks, turning back toward the horses. "You're the new ${title}. Whole place is yours, assumin' the bandits don't gut us 'fore we get there."

He reaches behind him, and the hatch snaps shut.

The wood slides open with a clack. The driver looks back. "What now?"

"Let's get one thing straight," you say. "I'm a ${title}, and you are a driver. Don't shut the door in my face. I wasn't done speaking."

He stares at you for a long second. The carriage hits a deep rut, shaking you both, but his grip on the reins doesn't slip.

"Beggin' your pardon, [i]my {lord}[/i]," he says, apparently taking extra effort to ensure you hear the disrespect. "I'll be sure to mind my manners while I try to keep the horses on the road and our throats uncut. How can I help you, ${title}?"

You stare at each other for a long time, and you realize you don't actually know what else to say.

"Well, I'm done now," you say finally.

The driver continues staring as the rain beats down on him for several moments more. "Very good," he says, and closes the hatch again.

You sure showed him.

*label broken_tree

The carriage gives a violent lurch, throwing you forward against the opposite bench. The iron wheels screech in the mud. Outside, the horses whinny in protest as the whole rig grinds to a sudden halt.

You catch your balance and wipe a circle of condensation off the grimy window. Through the rain and the gloom of the trees, you spot the problem. A massive, splintered pine log stretches dead across the road.

How incredibly cliché. Highway bandits? You hope the developers at least programmed them to do something interesting. You pat down your clothes, checking your belt and pockets for a weapon. Nothing.

You knock on the wooden hatch and it slides open. The driver's face is tight with tension.

"That log in the road," you say. "That's an ambush, right? Bandits?"

"Naw, the trees 'round here just like takin' naps in the mud," he mutters, his eyes darting toward the dense brush. He hauls a heavy-looking crossbow onto his lap. The gears click sharply as he cranks the string back. "Course it's bandits. Keep your head down, my {lord}. 'Less you feel like catchin' an arrow with your teeth."
